Yes—**a “Break-Even Autopilot in 7 days” offer can be realistic**, but only if you define the Day-7 outcome correctly and design the system to work under “messy books” conditions.

The trap is promising **perfect** break-even precision (requires clean job costing, correct classifications, consistent payroll treatment, WIP discipline, etc.). The opportunity is that **break-even is primarily an overhead + cash burn math problem**, and you can compute a _decision-grade_ break-even “floor” fast—even when the books are ugly—by using bank, payroll, and a controlled 80/20 data model.

1) Reality check: what’s realistic in 7 days vs. what isn’t
-----------------------------------------------------------

### Realistic in 7 days (even with messy books)

You can deliver:

1.  **Overhead run-rate (TTM/last 90 days) + burn rate**
2.  **Break-even _range_ and a minimum viable “price floor”**
3.  A **simple scoreboard** that shows the “silent turnovers” (where profit is being fumbled)
4.  A **guardrail** that prevents under-recovery of overhead going forward (via estimating/CRM template logic or a quoting rule)

This is enough to stop the 400-yards / lose-by-30 problem.

### Not realistic in 7 days (if books are a mess)

*   **True job-level margin by job type, crew, estimator** (requires consistent job costing and clean coding)
*   **Precise WIP and accrual-based gross margin accuracy**

Those are **Phase 2 (30–60 days)** outcomes.

**So the offer is realistic if Day 7 = “stop the bleeding with a defendable break-even floor and guardrails,”** not “your financials are now pristine.”

3) How to beat “junk in, junk out” in a week
--------------------------------------------

You do it by **not depending on their chart of accounts quality**.

### The key design choice: build a “shadow overhead ledger”

Instead of trusting their messy books, you compute overhead from:

*   **Bank and card transactions** (ground truth cash)
*   **Payroll totals** (ground truth labor burden)
*   **Debt service / rent / insurance / vehicles** (ground truth fixed obligations)

You then map spending into a **simple, stable overhead taxonomy** (10–20 buckets), using:

*   Vendor rules (Home Depot, ABC Supply, Beacon, fuel, insurance carriers, etc.)
*   Payroll classification (office vs field if possible; if not, assume conservative overhead until refined)
*   AI-assisted categorization + exception review by your team (not the roofer)

This yields a usable overhead run-rate fast, even if their QBO is chaos.

### Why this works

Break-even is:

**Break-even revenue = Overhead ÷ Gross margin %**

Even if gross margin % is uncertain, you can:

*   Use **last 90–180 days invoice vs. material spend proxy**
*   Use **industry margin bands** as a temporary sensitivity range
*   Output **a break-even range** (e.g., “You must collect $X–$Y/month to not lose money”)

That alone is a major “turnover reduction” lever.

4) What “Autopilot” actually means operationally
------------------------------------------------

If you want it to be _truly done-for-them_, you need two systems:

*   Pull bank/card feeds, payroll summaries, AR/AP snapshots

*   Refresh scoreboard automatically

Practical implementations:

1.  **Estimating template injection**: an “Overhead Recovery Line” auto-added based on overhead run-rate and capacity assumptions.
2.  **Quote floor rule**: minimum acceptable price per square / per crew-day / per job type, updated weekly.
3.  **Internal exception alerting**: you get notified when quotes/jobs fall below the floor; roofer keeps operating normally.

If the roofer doesn’t want to change pricing behavior, your system must **change the math underneath** the existing workflow.
